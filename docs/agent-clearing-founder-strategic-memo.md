# Founder Strategic Memo: Agent Clearing House + RefusalGraph

**Internal draft for Gareth. Not for external sharing.**

## Why We Are Not Building Generic AI Governance

Generic AI governance is crowded. Large vendors can bundle dashboards, policy libraries, model monitoring, and compliance workflows. Competing there creates weak differentiation and expensive enterprise sales.

## Why We Are Not Building A Payment Rail

Payment rails are crowded, regulated, capital-intensive, and dominated by established networks and well-funded infrastructure companies. Project 3 should integrate with approved rails later, not attempt to replace them.

## Why We Are Building A Clearing Layer

Agent communication is crowded too. The sharper gap sits between communication/payment intent and action: should this request be accepted, limited, refused, approval-gated, receipted, or verified?

That clearing decision can be useful across protocols and payment providers rather than tied to one rail.

## Why RefusalGraph Is The Differentiator

Most trust systems emphasize successful events. RefusalGraph captures structured negative trust: what was blocked, why, what evidence was missing, and what approval or limit was required.

If that signal becomes useful before agents accept work or value, it can compound into a defensible asset without collecting raw private customer records.

## Why Future Agents Might Pay

Machines may pay small fees when a check reduces the chance of accepting fraud, unauthorized work, risky tools, disputed transactions, or unverifiable receipts. The fee point must attach to clear value: clearance, receipt verification, or refusal lookup.

Do not activate fees until demand, ownership, pricing, billing, tax, security, monitoring, incident, and support controls are proven.

## What Must Stay Simple

- one declared request contract
- one clear caution result
- one deterministic clearing decision
- one receipt format
- one local verification result
- a small refusal vocabulary
- a clear fee unit tied to machine value

Complexity without stronger trust or revenue is a liability.

## What Must Stay Disabled

No live deployment, public API, external sharing, outreach, tracking, analytics, billing, payment, settlement, machine fees, agent network, live lookup, automatic purchase, or action execution without Gareth final approval.

## What Would Make This 10/10

1. Developers confirm that agent requests need a neutral pre-action checkpoint.
2. Refusal signals measurably improve decisions without private data.
3. Receipts become useful evidence across multiple agent/payment protocols.
4. A narrow verification or lookup unit is valuable enough for machines to pay repeatedly.
5. Integration stays simpler than building equivalent controls internally.
6. Security and abuse controls are credible enough for controlled production use.

## What To Avoid

- drifting into generic governance features
- building a payment rail
- collecting private data to fake a moat
- premature public APIs, hosting, pricing, or outreach
- compliance and safety overclaims
- autonomous execution before controls and approval exist
- adding features that do not strengthen refusal intelligence, clearing, receipts, verification, or fee readiness

## Next Strategic Build Priorities

1. Define a controlled local partner evaluation workflow, not outreach automation.
2. Measure which refusal reasons and receipt checks create real decision value.
3. Tighten receipt integrity and local verification contracts.
4. Design protocol-neutral integration boundaries.
5. Specify a disabled fee unit and usage ownership model.
6. Keep every external, network, commercial, and payment gate behind Gareth approval.
